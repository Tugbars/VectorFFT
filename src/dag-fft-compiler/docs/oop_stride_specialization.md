# Register pressure, M-project, and OOP stride specialization

This note records an investigation into the radix-32 codelets used by the
32x32 one-call OOP engine: whether they spill more than they should, how their
register pressure compares to FFTW, and what actually made them faster. It ends
with a generator feature (`--oop-strides`) and a specialized engine
(`engine_natural_oop_onecall_spec.c`) that beats the general path by 6 to 10
percent.

The short version: the codelets were never the naive monolithic form, they were
already 2-pass blocked and fit in registers per pass. The real win was not a
smarter spill or fuse strategy, it was removing the runtime stride arguments by
baking them as compile-time constants.

## 1. The starting question

The radix-32 leaf (n1) and twiddle (t1p) codelets each show on the order of 160
stack moves in their compiled object. The question was whether that exceeds the
"theoretical max", and how it compares to FFTW's radix-32 register pressure.

To measure peak register pressure directly, the generator has
`Regalloc.peak_live_analysis`, which walks the scheduled DAG and reports the
maximum number of simultaneously live values. It is gated behind
`VFFT_PEAK_LIVE=1` (stderr print). For radix-32, AVX-512, 32-ZMM budget:

| radix-32 n1 construction | peak_live (vs 32) | result |
|---|---|---|
| monolithic (one flat DAG) | 120 | OVERFLOW, 3.75x budget |
| 2-pass blocked, PASS 1 | 17 | FITS |
| 2-pass blocked, PASS 2 | 10 | FITS |

The monolithic path is the one that spills catastrophically: 120 values want to
be live, only 32 registers exist, so the codelet drowns in stack traffic (660
vmovapd, 381 of them stack spills). This is the "way above theoretical" case.
But the codelets we actually ship do not use it. The OOP emitter calls
`Dft.dft_expand_n1_blocked` (and `dft_expand_twiddled_spill` for t1), which is
the 2-pass Cooley-Tukey construction that emits a spill-marker boundary between
PASS 1 and PASS 2. Each pass independently fits in registers (17 and 10 live).
You can confirm the shipped OOP codelet is blocked: its source declares
`spill_re[32]` / `spill_im[32]` and has the PASS 1 / PASS 2 split.

So radix-32 = CT(4,8), and the 2-pass structure is what tames the pressure. The
documented monolithic peak_live of 260 in `dft.ml` is stale from an earlier DAG;
the current measured value is 120, still an overflow.

## 2. The FFTW comparison

FFTW's radix-32 no-twiddle codelet `n1fv_32` is generated by genfft with
`-variables 4`, an explicit bound on the number of live variables its scheduler
keeps. Compiled the same way (`-O3 -march=native -mavx512f`), it shows 36 stack
moves and 92 total vmovapd. So:

| radix-32 n1, AVX-512 | stack spills | total vmovapd |
|---|---|---|
| FFTW n1fv_32 (`-variables 4`) | 36 | 92 |
| VectorFFT monolithic | 381 | 660 |
| VectorFFT 2-pass blocked (fuse=0) | 107 | 265 |

The remaining gap between our blocked codelet (107) and FFTW (36) is not
register pressure. Our per-pass peak_live (17, 10) already fits, so within a
pass nothing is forced to spill. The 107 stack moves are the structural
pass-boundary store and reload: PASS 1's 32 complex outputs go to the spill
arrays and PASS 2 reloads them. FFTW never builds a hard boundary; its
cache-oblivious scheduler interleaves the two passes and consumes PASS 1 outputs
as they are produced, so its live window stays small and it spills only 36. That
interleave, not a tighter allocator, is the source of FFTW's lower count.

## 3. M-project: keeping pass-boundary values in registers

The generator has a mechanism aimed exactly at the pass-boundary store/reload,
called M-project in the `dft.ml` notes. It is the `fuse` parameter on
`Emit_c.make_spill_info ~ct:(n1,n2) ~fuse:M`. For CT(n1, n2) it keeps the last M
of the n2 PASS-2 sub-DFTs register-resident across the boundary (the
latest-stored, first-consumed values) instead of round-tripping them through the
spill arrays. The mechanics are: forward-declare those tags at outer scope,
assign them in PASS 1 with no declarator, skip their spill store, skip their
PASS 2 reload.

This was wired into the in-place (non-OOP) emitter but was a no-op on the OOP
path: `codelet_oop.ml` hardcoded `~fuse:0`, and even forcing fuse > 0 changed
nothing because the OOP PASS1/PASS2 emission never checked `is_fused_slot`. The
keep-in-register logic lived only in `emit_c.ml`.

We ported it. The OOP `emit_body_spill` now: builds an `is_fused_tag` predicate
from the spill_info, forward-declares fused tags before PASS 1, emits them with
`render_node_def ~no_declarator:true` and no spill store, and skips their reload
in PASS 2. The `--fuse N` flag now drives both emitters (via
`Codelet_oop.current_oop_fuse`). Result for radix-32, fuse=8:

| | non-OOP | OOP leaf | OOP t1p |
|---|---|---|---|
| fuse=0 vmovapd | 265 | 342 | 351 |
| fuse=8 vmovapd | 238 | 321 | 324 |
| saved | 27 | 21 | 27 |

Correct in all cases (relerr ~8e-15, unchanged). So M-project works, but the
saving is modest, around 20 to 27 vmovapd, and on its own it stayed under the
VM's descheduling noise in engine timing.

## 4. Why M-project helps OOP less, and what the real lever is

At the source level, fuse=8 collapses the same spill structure in both OOP and
in-place: 130 spill-array references drop to 2. The fusion is identical. The cap
on realized benefit is the register file. With 32 ZMM, the 32 inter-pass values
plus PASS-2's working set cannot all stay resident, so gcc re-spills the
overflow to its own stack. The realized saving is only the few values that fit
in the free registers.

This raised the question of whether OOP's runtime stride arguments (in_leg,
in_group, out_leg, out_group, all live across the butterfly) were stealing the
registers fusion needed. We tested it with a 2x2 over (stride encoding) x (fuse),
baking the engine's known strides as compile-time constants:

```
radix-32 OOP leaf, vmovapd
                 fuse=0   fuse=8
runtime strides   342      321      (fusion: -21)
compile-time str  304      283      (fusion: -21)
                  (-38)    (-38)     (strides: -38)
```

The two effects are independent and additive. Fusion saves the same 21 in both
stride regimes, and stride specialization saves the same 38 in both fuse
regimes. So the strides were not competing with fusion. They are a separate
overhead worth nearly twice as much (-38 vs -21): with compile-time strides the
`leg * in_leg_stride` address arithmetic folds to constant displacements, and
the four argument registers are freed. The reason fusion is slightly weaker in
OOP (21 vs 27 in-place) is the OOP-only live state that does not fold away: the
output-lane accumulators, base pointers, and the loop, not the strides.

The conclusion reframed the original goal. An OOP-specific fuse is not worth
building. The OOP-specific lever is stride specialization.

## 5. The `--oop-strides` feature

Added a generator flag `--oop-strides L,G,OL,OG`. When set, the OOP emitter
drops the four stride parameters from the signature and bakes them as
`const size_t` at the top of the function body. The body already references the
strides by name, so the change is localized to the signature emission. The
symbol gets a `_spec` suffix so specialized and general codelets can coexist.
M-project rides along unchanged (`--fuse N`).

The 32x32 one-call engine uses two stride configs, so it needs two specialized
codelet shapes (plus the log3 twiddle variant):

```
leaf n1 : --oop-strides 256,1,8,32     (R*V, 1, V, R for R=32, V=8)
t1p     : --oop-strides 256,1,256,1    (in-place twiddle stage)
```

Regenerate with `codelets/regen_spec_r32.sh`. The specialized codelets take a
7-argument ABI ending in `me` (no stride args).

## 6. Results

Measured by linking the general and `_spec` codelets into one binary and timing
them interleaved (so the VM's whole-run descheduling spikes hit both equally and
cancel in the ratio), FFTW PATIENT, rdtsc min-of-120, stable across repeated
runs:

| | spec / general | vs FFTW (general then spec) |
|---|---|---|
| flat, K=512 | 0.93 to 0.95 | 1.02x then 1.08 to 1.15x |
| flat, K=1024 | 0.90 to 0.95 | 1.02x then 1.13 to 1.18x |
| log3, K=512 | 0.89 to 0.94 | 1.06x then 1.16 to 1.23x |
| log3, K=1024 | 0.92 to 0.96 | 1.06x then 1.14 to 1.21x |

Specialized plus fused is consistently 6 to 10 percent faster than the general
codelets, with identical numerics (relerr 8e-15). The recommended log3 path now
beats FFTW by roughly 1.15 to 1.23x, up from about 1.06 to 1.15x. At the codelet
level the leaf went from 342 to 283 vmovapd. Essentially all of the runtime gain
is the stride specialization, not M-project, but the two are additive and both
are on in the shipped `_spec` codelets.

## 7. Store-on-compute: the in-place versus OOP spill gap

A direct spill comparison of the radix-32 leaf showed the in-place codelet at 107
stack moves and the OOP codelet at 162, about 52 percent more, despite identical
per-pass register pressure (peak_live 17 and 10) and identical spill arrays. The
gap was not the buffer mode (in-place versus out-of-place buffer is bit-identical
on spills) and not the strides (specialization folds those but the stack count
stays). It was the output pattern: the UnitGroup store scatters outputs at a
non-unit group stride, so the emitter accumulated all 32 complex results into
`out_lane_*` registers (160 references) and wrote them in a separate phase at the
end, holding 64 values live across the tail. The in-place codelet writes each
output to memory the instant it is computed and holds nothing.

`--oop-store-fused` makes the UnitGroup path do the same: store on compute. It is
safe because the load phase pulls every input into registers before the body
runs, and in the 2-pass codelets PASS 2 reads only the spill arrays, never the
output buffer, so storing an output (even in-place) cannot clobber anything still
needed. UnitLeg store is unaffected (its transpose operates on `out_lane`). The
effect on the radix-32 leaf, on top of specialization and fuse:

| radix-32 leaf | stack spills | vmovapd |
|---|---|---|
| shipped OOP (fuse=0) | 162 | 342 |
| + M-project fuse=8 | 162 | 321 |
| + stride specialization | 162 | 283 |
| + store-on-compute | 90 | 189 |
| (in-place reference) | 107 | 238 |

The optimized OOP leaf now sits below the in-place codelet on both metrics.
relerr unchanged at 8e-15. Engine effect (interleaved): the flat path gains 2 to
9 percent; the log3 path is neutral because it is compute-bound on its 95 FMAs,
so the store side is already hidden. The shipped `_spec` codelets now include all
three optimizations (regenerated by `codelets/regen_spec_r32.sh`).

Two related ideas were evaluated and not pursued. Interleaving PASS 1 and PASS 2
(FFTW's approach, which is why FFTW spills only 36) would shrink the spill arrays
themselves, but it is a rewrite of the OOP emitter's per-pass scheduler (the
existing bisection scheduler does not reach that path) and store-on-compute
already put us below the in-place codelet, so the payoff is uncertain without
core-versus-memory profiling on real hardware. Finer blocking reduces to the
same concurrency question and is already captured by M-project fuse, which bites
harder now that store-on-compute freed the `out_lane` registers (with it on,
fuse=0 to fuse=8 goes 219 to 189 vmovapd, versus 304 to 283 before).

The downstream effect of this spill work on the MKL comparison is in
`docs/mkl_comparison.md`.

## 8. Honest caveats

- This does not close the gap to Intel MKL on OOP. MKL wins OOP by 15 to 35
  percent, and that comes from hand-written assembly and the inherent
  out-of-place memory penalty, not from codelet register pressure. A 6 to 10
  percent codelet win is a different lever and will not catch MKL on OOP by
  itself.
- The magnitude was extracted on a noisy single-vCPU VM using interleaving. The
  interleaved spec-versus-general ratio is the reliable signal; a standalone
  run's absolute FFTW ratio bounces by 10 percent or more between runs and
  should not be trusted in isolation. Re-confirm on quiet hardware.
- On AVX2 (16 YMM) the register math that specialization frees up is different
  and the margin could shift. The codelets here are AVX-512; regenerate with
  `--isa avx2 --oop-strides ...` and re-measure on the AVX2 deploy target.
