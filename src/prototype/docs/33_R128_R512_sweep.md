# 33. R=128 → R=512 Monolithic Codelet Sweep: Finding the Crossover

## Context

After [doc 32](32_of_expr_memo.md) removed the O(N⁴) IR-pipeline wall,
generating R=128, R=256, and R=512 monolithic codelets became feasible.
This opened the empirical question: how far does the monolithic-codelet
strategy extend before chained smaller codelets start to win?

This doc records the sweep. The short answer: monolithic wins decisively
through R=256, the curve flattens at R=512 with a clean crossover at
B=128, and the OoO engine isn't what runs out first — the front-end and
store buffer do.

## What got wired

Three new picker entries in `lib/dft.ml`:

| N | Picker entry | Gen time | Vec instr | Compile time | Object size |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 128 | `Cooley_Tukey (8, 16)`   | 0.21s | 3318  | 3.8s  | ~67 KB |
| 256 | `Cooley_Tukey (16, 16)`  | 0.64s | 7617  | 5.5s  | ~125 KB |
| 512 | `Cooley_Tukey (16, 32)`  | 2.58s | 17318 | 21.4s | ~282 KB |

All three pass prime correctness (56/56) and execution sanity (finite
outputs, no NaN). R=128 and R=256 use balanced-ish factorizations
extending the pattern from R=64 (`(8, 8)`). R=512's choice is discussed
below — initial pick was `(8, 64)` on AVX-512 lane-alignment reasoning,
but the data didn't support that intuition.

## R=128 monolithic vs work-equivalent multi-stage

For each batch size B, we ran the new R=128 monolithic codelet on K=B
batches and compared against four multi-stage chains processing the same
data volume: 8×16, 16×8, 4×32, 32×4. The multi-stage path executes
codelet-A → inline AVX-512 twiddle pass → codelet-B. Times in ns per
DFT-128 batch.

| B    | Mono   | 8×16   | 16×8   | 4×32   | 32×4   | Best multi / mono |
|------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|---|
| 8    | 1572   | 1316   | 1333   | 1498   | 1459   | 0.82× (multi wins) |
| 16   | 3500   | 3979   | 4054   | 4590   | 4436   | 1.14 (mono +14%) |
| 32   | 7559   | 10695  | 10677  | 10146  | 10129  | 1.31 (mono +31%) |
| 64   | 14362  | 25432  | 25171  | 19880  | 19789  | 1.39 (mono +39%) |
| 128  | 28634  | 53109  | 52199  | 49937  | 49111  | 1.74 (mono +74%) ← peak |
| 256  | 72918  | 135139 | 125892 | 118730 | 121942 | 1.64 (mono +64%) |
| 512  | 232162 | 299096 | 301718 | 315004 | 294361 | 1.31 (mono +31%) |
| 1024 | 479617 | 683528 | 664168 | 684550 | 706700 | 1.43 (mono +43%) |

Monolithic wins for all B ≥ 16. The peak advantage is at B=128 (+74%).
The B=8 anomaly is interesting: multi-stage wins by 18%. Most plausible
cause is L1 instruction-cache amortization — R=128's codelet body is
~3300 vector instructions; at B=8 the body runs only 8 times per call
and the icache miss cost on first warmup isn't paid back. Multi-stage
uses smaller codelet bodies that fit cleanly in icache. Not confirmed
with hardware counters yet.

8×16 ≈ 16×8 and 4×32 ≈ 32×4. Factor order doesn't materially affect
multi-stage runtime, just the factor pair. 8×16 is best overall —
likely because R=8 and R=16 are register-resident (no spill recipe
needed) while R=32 has explicit spill traffic.

## R=256 monolithic vs multi-stage

Same harness shape, extended factorizations.

| B   | Mono   | 16×16  | 8×32   | 32×8   | 4×64   | 64×4   | Best multi / mono |
|-----|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|---|
| 8   | 3618   | 3021   | 3429   | 3369   | 3807   | 3799   | 0.84× (multi wins) |
| 16  | 7634   | 9484   | 9592   | 9608   | 9128   | 9140   | 1.20 (mono +20%) |
| 32  | 15008  | 20614  | 19932  | 20007  | 18792  | 18709  | 1.25 (mono +25%) |
| 64  | 28828  | 41509  | 43944  | 43927  | 38179  | 38042  | 1.32 (mono +32%) |
| 128 | 60039  | 90688  | 109640 | 101859 | 98872  | 98300  | 1.51 (mono +51%) ← peak |
| 256 | 186546 | 245594 | 279013 | 279373 | 273805 | 274876 | 1.32 (mono +32%) |
| 512 | 473482 | 529289 | 602401 | 607857 | 624464 | 606311 | 1.12 (mono +12%) |

Same qualitative shape as R=128 but with smaller margins. Peak advantage
shifted slightly later but shrank from +74% to +51%. The curve flattens
at the large-B end significantly — only +12% advantage at B=512 vs R=128's
+31% at the same B. Indicates we're approaching the crossover.

At compute-dominated B (16-64), 4×64 / 64×4 wins among multi-stage —
this is where R=64 (our most-tuned existing codelet) earns its keep.
At large B, balanced 16×16 dominates again. The pattern carries forward.

## R=512: the crossover

This is where the strategy flips. Same harness with factorizations
8×64, 64×8, 16×32, 32×16, 4×128, 128×4.

| B   | Mono    | 8×64    | 64×8    | 16×32   | 32×16   | 4×128   | 128×4   | Best multi / mono |
|-----|---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|---|
| 8   | 7571    | 9351    | 9562    | 8458    | 8370    | 8617    | 8515    | 1.11 (mono +11%) |
| 16  | 15532   | 18846   | 18996   | 17023   | 16989   | 16777   | 16844   | 1.08 (mono +8%) |
| 32  | 31636   | 37590   | 37773   | 38728   | 38636   | 32209   | 32193   | 1.02 (near parity) |
| 64  | 75965   | 91169   | 90730   | 82869   | 83129   | 80981   | 78858   | 1.04 (mono +4%) |
| 128 | 247346  | 271014  | 273802  | 221043  | 231442  | 332962  | 333963  | **0.89 (multi +12%)** |
| 256 | 893110  | 673723  | 722761  | 600622  | 552702  | 991154  | 978567  | **0.62 (multi +61%)** |
| 512 | 1907476 | 1537685 | 1652174 | 1253433 | 1189461 | 2035239 | 2043892 | **0.62 (multi +61%)** |

Monolithic wins through B=64 but loses decisively at B ≥ 128. The pattern
is qualitatively different from R=128 / R=256:

- **The peak monolithic advantage shrank to +11% at small B** (vs +74%
  for R=128 at B=128). Even where mono wins, the margin is small.
- **The flip is sharp.** From B=64 (mono +4%) to B=128 (multi +12%) is
  one binary step — multi-stage doesn't gradually catch up, it crosses
  cleanly.
- **At B ≥ 256, multi-stage wins by 1.5×+.** Monolithic is comprehensively
  worse, not marginally.

Best multi-stage choice also varies with B. At small B (≤ 64), the
4×128 / 128×4 chains (using our new R=128 codelet as a stage) come
closest to monolithic — within 2% at B=32. At large B (≥ 128), balanced
16×32 / 32×16 dominates. The transition reflects which size has the
right ILP-vs-codesize tradeoff for the regime.

## The 8×64 hypothesis: empirically refuted

The initial picker entry for R=512 was `Cooley_Tukey (8, 64)`, motivated
by:

- AVX-512 SIMD width is 8 doubles, matching radix-8 perfectly
- R=64 is our most-tuned existing codelet (doc 9: beats hand by 33%)
- "One big + one small" concentrates work in the well-validated R=64
- W_512^{8k} powers reduce to W_64^k, aligning inner-loop twiddles

All four arguments are theoretically sound. None of them held empirically.
The multi-stage column comparison (8×64 vs 16×32 ratios at every B):

| B   | 8×64 / mono | 16×32 / mono | 16×32 better than 8×64 by |
|-----|---|---|---|
| 8   | 1.24 | 1.12 | 11% |
| 16  | 1.21 | 1.10 | 10% |
| 32  | 1.19 | 1.22 | (8×64 slightly better) |
| 64  | 1.20 | 1.09 | 10% |
| 128 | 1.10 | 0.89 | 19% |
| 256 | 0.75 | 0.67 | 11% |
| 512 | 0.81 | 0.66 | 19% |

16×32 consistently beats 8×64 by 10-19%. The factors that overrode the
lane-alignment argument:

- **R=64's stack-op cost.** 263 stack ops per K iteration in R=64's
  inner body, vs 84 in R=32. Multiplied by K iterations, the
  spill-memory traffic adds up.
- **Reorder buffer fit.** R=32's body (591 FP-instr) fits closer to the
  OoO scheduler's effective window (~150-300 instructions) than R=64's
  body (1485 FP-instr). The OoO engine can keep more of R=32's body
  "live" simultaneously, finding more ILP per cycle.
- **Pipeline balance.** 16×32 has two moderate-cost stages; 8×64 has
  one heavy (R=64) and one light (R=8). Uneven pipelines don't fill
  execution ports as densely.

We re-tested by changing the monolithic R=512 picker entry from
CT(8, 64) to CT(16, 32) and benchmarking only the monolithic side. The
two variants ran within 1-3% of each other — algsimp is a strong enough
normalizer that the choice of internal factorization barely affects the
monolithic codelet's runtime (consistent with [doc 31](31_split_radix_research_arc.md)'s
finding that algsimp normalizes CT vs SR choice). We kept CT(16, 32)
for the picker entry, matching the empirically winning multi-stage shape.

The lesson generalizes: **architectural intuitions about a single
resource (AVX-512 lane width) don't reliably predict end-to-end runtime
when many resources interact.** The right factorization is empirical.

## Asm-level scheduling: the structural mechanism

Why does monolithic win at R=128 / R=256 but lose at R=512? Inspecting
the asm makes the mechanism concrete.

**Monolithic R=128 main loop body:** between labels `.L3` and `.L8`,
GCC emits **5863 instructions in one straight-line block** (281 FMAs,
659 stack ops). The whole body is one scheduling region — GCC has full
visibility across all 5863 instructions and interleaves butterflies
freely. A typical 30-instruction window from inside the body shows
vmul/vfma/vadd/vsub patterns operating on independent register pairs,
with stack stores placed by the spill recipe at recipe-planned intervals.

**Multi-stage 8×16 wrapper asm:** the wrapper compiled from
`multistage_wrapper.c` shows the structure plainly:

```
multistage_8x16:
    ...
    call radix16_t1_dit_fwd_avx512_gen_inplace_su_spill@PLT   ← BARRIER 1
    ...
    vmulpd, vfmsub231pd, vmovapd ...  (11-line inlined twiddle loop)
    ...
    vzeroupper
    pop rbx; pop r12; ...; pop rbp
    jmp radix8_t1_dit_fwd_avx512_gen_inplace_su_spill@PLT     ← BARRIER 2
```

Three separate scheduling regions, two hard barriers. At each barrier:
callee-saved register push/pop, `vzeroupper`, register restores from
rsp slots, PLT indirection.

Per DFT-128 batch of B, the arithmetic work is nearly identical:

| | Monolithic | Multi-stage 8×16 |
|---|---|---|
| Arith ops per batch | 2378 | 2448 (+3%) |
| FMAs per batch | 281 | 352 (+25%) |
| Total instr @ B=128 | 750k | 740k |

Multi-stage isn't doing meaningfully more work. The 74% wall-time gap
at B=128 is purely scheduling and memory-traffic structure.

**Five concrete mechanisms behind the gap:**

The first is lost cross-stage scheduling visibility — GCC schedules R=16
without knowledge of what follows, then the twiddle loop independently,
then R=8 independently. Independent FMAs across the boundary can't
interleave because they sit on opposite sides of a `call` instruction.

The second is forced memory traffic at boundaries. Stage 1 outputs must
be written before stage 2 reads them. The `call` instruction acts as
a memory barrier; values that should stay in registers commit to memory
regardless.

The third is OoO engine retire-and-restart at each function call. The
reorder buffer (224 entries on SKX) drains at each barrier. After return,
the engine refills from scratch with the next function's instructions.

The fourth is spill-recipe non-coordination. Monolithic's recipe places
all 659 stack ops with global DAG visibility. Multi-stage uses two
separate recipes (R=16's 12 stack ops, R=8's 0), each making local
decisions; plus the function call boundary forces additional caller-saved
register spills neither recipe planned for.

The fifth is function-call overhead. Each PLT call is roughly 30-50
cycles of pure ABI overhead. Amortized into noise at B=128, but at B=8
it's a meaningful fraction of total work — which is partly why monolithic
loses at B=8 (here the icache cost on the larger codelet exceeds the
barrier savings).

## What ran out at R=512: front-end and store buffer, not the OoO scheduler

The natural framing is "the OoO engine ran out of steam at R=512" but
the actionable framing is more specific. The Skylake-X / SPR-class CPU
has fixed structural limits:

| Resource | SKX limit | SPR limit |
|---|---|---|
| Reorder buffer (ROB) | 224 | 512 |
| Scheduler / RS | 97 | 160 |
| Physical FP registers | 168 | 332 |
| Load buffer | 72 | 128 |
| Store buffer | 56 | 72 |
| L1 instruction cache | 32 KB | 64 KB |
| L1 µop cache | ~1.5K µops | ~1.5K µops |

For each codelet size, what fits and what overflows:

| R=N | body FP-instr | stack ops | asm bytes | front-end fit | store buffer fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 64  | 591    | 263  | ~50 KB  | µop cache marginal | yes |
| 128 | 3530   | 689  | ~67 KB  | icache fits (SKX 32KB tight, SPR 64KB OK) | yes (~40 in flight) |
| 256 | 8541   | 2099 | 125 KB  | icache overflows on SKX | tight (~50-70 in flight) |
| 512 | 19590  | 5141 | 282 KB  | icache overflows on both, µop cache nowhere close | **overflows (~75 in flight)** |

The decisive evidence for *which* resource gives out first is the
direction of the curve as B grows. For R=512, the multi/mono ratio gets
**worse** (from monolithic's perspective) as B grows: 1.04 at B=64 →
0.89 at B=128 → 0.62 at B=256. If pure OoO scheduling were the issue,
bigger batches would amortize the cost and the curve should improve, not
worsen. Instead we see structural ceilings — icache, µop cache, store
buffer — that compound rather than amortize with more work.

Quantitatively at R=512, B=256: the codelet body executes 256 times. Each
execution traverses 282 KB of instruction memory. L1 I-cache misses
compound across iterations because the body doesn't fit in either L1
I-cache (32-64 KB) or µop cache (~1.5K µops). The store buffer at 56
entries (SKX) sees 5141 stack ops per execution; with ~150-instruction
OoO visibility, roughly 75 stores are in flight at any moment — over
the 56-entry capacity. Each overflow stalls the front-end while a slot
frees.

R=128 doesn't show this signature because its body (3530 instructions,
689 stack ops) fits within these limits with margin. R=256 starts showing
flattening at B=512 — consistent with crossing some thresholds but not
all of them. R=512 crosses all of them simultaneously and the curve
flips hard.

This points at concrete attacks if larger monoliths are desired:

- **Reduce icache footprint.** GCC emits many sequential
  `vmovapd reg, reg` register moves; tighter register allocation could
  shave ~30% of those.
- **Reduce store-buffer pressure.** The 5141 stack ops at R=512 is the
  dominant memory cost. The current spill recipe may be over-spilling;
  a spill-controller redesign (deferred during SR work) could revisit.
- **Fragment the codelet into icache-sized hot regions.** The "cross-stage
  codelet fusion" path attacks this from the other direction — keep
  multi-stage's two function calls but inline them into one block that
  GCC can schedule together, getting the best of both.

None of this affects R=128 or R=256, which fit comfortably.

## Picker design implications

The data forces a new dimension into the picker. Where R ≤ 256 has a
clean (N → factorization) decision, R = 512 has (N, B → factorization):

| N | Recommendation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 64 | Monolithic (existing) | |
| 128 | Monolithic | Wins for B ≥ 16 by 14-74%, ship as default |
| 256 | Monolithic | Wins for B ≥ 16 by 12-64%, ship as default |
| 512 | **(N, B)-aware**: monolithic for B ≤ 64, multi-stage for B ≥ 128 | Crossover at B = 128 |
| 1024+ | Probably never monolithic on this microarch | Untested but trend predicts loss at most B |

For R = 512 specifically, the multi-stage choice is also B-dependent.
At small B (≤ 64) where monolithic still wins, the best multi-stage
fallback is 4×128 or 128×4 (uses our new R=128 codelet as a stage —
within 2% of monolithic at B=32). At large B where multi-stage wins
outright, balanced 16×32 / 32×16 dominates.

The planner currently picks factorizations by N alone. Extending to
(N, B) is straightforward: add a wisdom-format field for B-bucketed
plan choice, populated at install-time benchmarking. The R=512 cell
is the first time this becomes necessary; future R≥1024 work will
need it for every size.

## Caveats

Multi-stage results are a lower bound on real multi-stage cost. The
harness doesn't include the **data shuffling between stages** that a
semantically-correct Cooley-Tukey decomposition requires — strided
reads from input layout to produce sub-DFT inputs, and unshuffled
writes to output layout. The harness measures equivalent computational
work and inter-stage memory traffic only. Real multi-stage execution
pays additional shuffle cost (likely an extra memory pass), which
would shift the boundary further in favor of monolithic. The B=8
monolithic loss at R=128 / R=256 might disappear once shuffle cost is
included; the R=512 crossover at B=128 might shift to B=256.

A real-shuffle harness is worth building but wasn't required for the
sweep's main finding: **at R=512 the crossover exists and is in the
B=64-128 range; tighter measurement won't change the design implication**.

## Where this leaves us

Three new picker entries shipped with empirical justification.
Monolithic codelet generation now extends through R=512 in principle,
though R=512 should be used selectively. The OoO-engine framing for
where monoliths break down is now grounded in specific resources
(front-end, store buffer) that can be attacked individually.

Open work suggested by this sweep:

The first is wiring the (N, B)-aware planner — the R=512 entry needs
it to choose between monolithic and 16×32 / 32×16 multi-stage based on
batch. The data exists; the planner change is mechanical.

The second is the spill-controller redesign deferred during the SR
research arc. R=512's 5141 stack ops suggests over-spilling; reducing
to maybe 2500-3500 might make R=512 monolithic viable at all B by
keeping store-buffer pressure in bounds.

The third is the real-shuffle multi-stage harness — confirms the
monolithic boundary under fair comparison.

The fourth is hardware-counter validation of the icache / µop-cache /
store-buffer hypothesis at the R=512 crossover (`perf stat -e
L1-icache-load-misses,idq.dsb_uops,resource_stalls.sb`). The structural
explanation is consistent with the runtime curve shape but not yet
directly confirmed.

The fifth, separately, is cross-stage codelet fusion — attack the 21%
executor overhead from the VTune analysis from a different angle than
making codelets larger. This is the multi-week investment if larger
monoliths plateau.
