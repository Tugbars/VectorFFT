# 63 — V4 Estimate Method

**Status:** Landed in `src/prototype-core/estimate_plan.h` 2026-05-17. DP planner unchanged at `dp_planner.h`; V4 is a parallel path for cells without wisdom coverage.

**One-line summary:** A cost-model-driven plan picker that enumerates every factorization of N, scores each with a measurement-anchored cost model, returns the lowest-scoring plan. Runs in microseconds, picks plans within the recipe family wisdom encodes, and matches measured-best winners on most tested cells.

---

## 1 — Why an estimate method at all

The planning landscape has three options for picking a factorization at runtime:

1. **Wisdom lookup** — measured truth from prior calibration. Microseconds. Limited to the (N, K) cells you bothered to calibrate.
2. **DP planner** — recursive measurement of sub-plans with memoization. Seconds. Algorithmic, no prior calibration needed, but inherits known structural gaps (see §5).
3. **Estimate** — pure cost-model scoring with no runtime measurement. Microseconds. Always available regardless of wisdom coverage.

You always need the third one. No wisdom file covers every (N, K) a user might call with. Greedy factorize is the degenerate version of estimate; V4 is what estimate should be.

V4 is **not** a replacement for wisdom on cells where wisdom exists. It's the floor that runs when wisdom is silent.

---

## 2 — Why pure cost models hit a structural ceiling

This is the most important framing point, and it took a long discussion to crystallize.

### 2.1 CPE is measured, not derived

Every "cost model" worth using sits on top of measured codelet timings. The prototype's `radix_cpe.h` table is generated by `cost_model/measure_cpe.c` running each codelet (R × {n1, t1, t1s, log3} × {avx2, avx512}) standalone at several `me` sample points (256, 4096, 65536). The cost model is an *amortization layer* over CPE — it lets you skip from "I have per-codelet timings" to "I want per-plan cost" without paying measurement at every plan.

CPE itself is calibration data, not physics. The cost model inherits CPE's blind spots.

### 2.2 The isolated-CPE trap

CPE is measured with the codelet running standalone — single-stage plan, K-batched data, cold-cache start, no prior stage's cache state. Real plans have:

- Cache state inherited from the prior stage.
- Branch predictor history primed for a specific group-loop trip count.
- HW prefetcher partially warmed up at one stride about to be replaced by another.
- ROB occupancy that depends on the call chain.

None of these show up in isolated CPE. They're the residual "in-context" effects that flip variant rank between standalone bench and real plan measurement.

Concrete example: on R=64 at me=256 (Raptor Lake AVX-2):
- `cyc_t1s = 186.5` (lowest isolated)
- `cyc_log3 = 192.2`
- Production exhaustive picks **LOG3** in a real `[4,4,4,64]` plan. T1S loses in-context despite winning isolated by 3%.

### 2.3 MKL doesn't have a magical cost model

MKL's `DftiCommitDescriptor` runs in microseconds with no measurement, on cells you've never seen. It looks like a cost-model triumph. It isn't.

MKL ships a **decision tree** mapping `(N, K, strides, alignment, CPUID-uarch, thread-count)` to a precomputed plan, built from Intel's offline measurement campaigns on Intel silicon. When you upgrade MKL versions, part of what you're paying for is updated decision tables. It's the same "measure once, ship the result" pattern as a wisdom file — just done by the vendor at compile time instead of by the user at calibrate time.

The FFT community has had 25 years to build a first-principles cost model and the state of the art is still "measure, then cache." That's evidence about the problem's structure, not about effort.

### 2.4 The right framing: "approximately right within top-K"

The cost model's job isn't to **rank** plans correctly. It's to **shortlist** plans containing the optimum, with K small enough that measurement is cheap. If V4 puts the true winner at rank 13 out of 491 candidates, measuring top-15 is still 30× cheaper than exhaustive and lands on the right plan.

This sets a much weaker requirement than "rank-accurate." V4 meets it.

## 2.5 — Scope: memory-bound systems only

**V4 only works on memory-bound FFT workloads. On compute-bound systems the entire model fails to predict the best plan even remotely close.**

This is the deepest scope assumption baked into the method, and it's worth flagging explicitly before anyone reads the cost-term breakdown below.

### What's memory-bound vs compute-bound for FFT

Total arithmetic in an FFT is fixed by N: roughly `5 N log₂(N)` real FLOPs regardless of factorization. Total memory traffic depends on the **number of stages** — each stage is one full pass over the N×K buffer, so plans with more stages do more memory work for the same total arithmetic.

On a system where memory bandwidth is the bottleneck:
- Fewer stages = faster (less buffer traffic).
- Wide radixes at outer stages stall on memory; narrow radixes at outer stages keep ports fed.
- Working-set residency in cache dominates per-stage cost.

On a system where compute is the bottleneck:
- Stage count doesn't matter (same total FLOPs).
- Wide radixes win for arithmetic density (more FMA per dispatch).
- Cache residency is amortized away by OoO and prefetcher; only FMA pipe throughput matters.

V4's two new terms — `wide_penalty` and `buffer_pass_cost_per_stage` — both encode memory-bound physics directly. On a compute-bound system both would be approximately zero (memory is free), V4 would collapse back to "pick the multiset with the cheapest summed isolated CPE," and the picks would invert toward wide-radix-outer plans that V4 currently penalizes.

### When the memory-bound assumption holds

Practically every modern general-purpose CPU running FFT on N ≳ 1024 is memory-bound:

- **x86 desktop / server (Raptor Lake, EPYC, etc.)** — sustained FMA throughput ≫ DRAM bandwidth at common N×K sizes. The whole project's [memory-bound thesis](../../../../) is built on this.
- **ARM server (Graviton, Ampere)** — similar imbalance, slightly more compute headroom but DRAM still wins.
- **Apple M-series** — better memory bandwidth than Intel, but at sufficiently large N×K still memory-bound.

### When the memory-bound assumption breaks

- **GPU FFT** with HBM and SIMT execution — wildly different architecture, V4 doesn't apply at all. Not the target.
- **DSP / FPGA accelerators with tightly-coupled SRAM** — local memory is essentially free, compute-bound at all sizes. V4's structure inverts; need a separate cost model.
- **Tiny cells where N×K fits L1 entirely** — the cell itself is compute-bound regardless of host. V4 handles this *gracefully* via the `bytes_per_cycle[L1]` = 45 entry — when buffer fits L1, buffer-pass cost shrinks proportionally and arithmetic dominates the score. So V4 degrades correctly at small N×K, but it's no longer doing useful filtering at that point (the candidate space is small enough to enumerate exhaustively in microseconds anyway).
- **Plans where the buffer fully fits L2 AND the inner stages are all L1-resident** — borderline compute-bound regime. V4's wide-radix penalty still applies (loads still go through AGUs even from L1), but the buffer-pass term is small. Predictions degrade in accuracy here; this is where the cell-specific calibration misses (like N=1024 K=4) tend to surface.

### Why this matters for the doc

Every claim in §3-7 — the cost-term derivations, the "fewer stages wins" reasoning, the "wide-radix-outer is bad" recipe — is premised on memory being the bottleneck. If you port V4 to a system where compute dominates (or even one where compute and memory are balanced), the predictions will go off in unpredictable directions and the model will likely be worse than greedy factorize. Don't port V4 without verifying the host's memory regime first.

The right way to extend V4 to a compute-bound system would be to drop both new terms and rebuild from a compute-side model — different physics, different calibration constants, probably different model structure entirely. That's a separate workstream.

## 3 — The V4 method

V4 builds on the recalibrated V2 cost model (`src/prototype/cost_model/factorizer.h:stride_score_factorization_v2`) by adding two terms:

### 3.1 The full per-stage score formula

```
score_stage(s) = data_cost      (V2 baseline — bf_cost × cache_factor × work)
               + tw_cost        (V2 baseline — twiddle table memory traffic)
               + dtlb_cost      (V2 baseline — DTLB miss penalty per group)
               + wide_penalty   (NEW in V4 — wide-radix-outer load stall)
               + buffer_pass    (NEW in V4 — full buffer transit per stage)

total = Σ score_stage(s)
```

### 3.2 The wide-radix-outer penalty term

**Insight:** An R-codelet does R loads + R stores per butterfly. The codelet's CPE in isolation reflects its FMA chain + scheduler limits. When that codelet runs at a stride where its working set exceeds L1, the loads start stalling on L2/L3 latency. The wider the codelet, the more loads expose to that stall — proportional to `R - baseline_R`.

```
wide_penalty(R, ws_tier) = max(0, R - baseline_R)
                         × extra_latency(ws_tier)
                         × groups × K

where extra_latency = {L1: 0, L2: 7, L3: 35, DRAM: 150}  (cycles)
      baseline_R   = 4
```

R=4 codelets are the reference — their CPE was calibrated against their own load count, so they pay no penalty. R=16 at L2 tier pays `12 × 7 = 84 cycles × work`. R=64 at L3 pays `60 × 35 = 2100 cycles × work`.

**Effect:** Plans with wide radixes at outer stages (where ws > L1) get penalized hard. Plans with R=4 outer + wide inner get no penalty at all (wide-R at L1 has zero extra_latency). This *encodes* the recipe — small R outer, wide R innermost — without hard-coding it. The recipe becomes an emergent property of the model on hosts where the calibration holds.

### 3.3 The per-stage buffer-pass term

**Insight:** Each stage is a full pass over the N×K complex buffer (read every element, write every element). Between adjacent stages the buffer transits cache. That transit costs `2 × buffer_bytes / effective_bandwidth_at_buffer_tier` cycles. Five stages pay five transits; four stages pay four. This directly penalizes stage count.

```
buffer_pass_cost = 2 × N × K × 16  bytes
                  / bytes_per_cycle(buffer_tier)

where bytes_per_cycle = {L1: 45, L2: 14, L3: 7, DRAM: 9}
      (Raptor Lake @ 5.7 GHz: L1~256 GB/s, L2~80 GB/s, L3~40 GB/s, DDR5~50 GB/s)

charged once per stage.
```

**Effect:** A 4-stage `[4,4,4,16]` and a 5-stage `[4,4,4,4,4]` differ by one buffer pass. On N=1024 K=128 (buffer = 1 MB in L2 tier), that's `2 × 1MB / 14 = 150K cycles` of difference — enough to tip the lower-stage-count plan ahead. This is the term that distinguishes plans of the same family within V4.

### 3.4 The cache_factor / CPE / DTLB / twiddle pieces inherited from V2

These are unchanged from prototype V2's recalibration (see doc 58):

- `cache_factor`: tier multipliers 1.0 / 1.4 / 2.3 / 4.0 for L1 / L2 / L3 / DRAM working-set residency.
- `bf_cost`: per-radix CPE, me-swept across 3 sample points, **min over available variants** per stage (n1 for stage 0, min(t1, t1s, log3) for inner stages).
- DTLB: penalty when `pages_per_group > dtlb_entries` (Raptor Lake ~96 entries × 4 KB).
- Twiddle memory traffic: `(R-1) × accumulated_K × 16` bytes per stage, with extra penalty if it spills L1.
- L3 buffer floor: cache_factor floored to 4.0 when full buffer > L3 (DRAM bound).

### 3.5 Factorization enumeration

For each `(N, K)`, recursively walk every ordered factorization of N into available radixes (n1 + t1s required for inner-stage usability). Up to `STRIDE_MAX_STAGES` = 16 stages deep. Each leaf factorization is scored; the lowest score wins. For N=1024 this enumerates ~491 candidates. For N=4096 K=4: ~1869. Sub-millisecond total scoring time.

## 4 — Calibration parameters

V4 has two sets of constants that need per-host calibration:

| Constant | What it represents | Raptor Lake @ 5.7 GHz value | How to re-probe |
|---|---|---|---|
| `bytes_per_cycle[L1,L2,L3,DRAM]` | Cache-tier streaming bandwidth | 45 / 14 / 7 / 9 | Small probe doing streaming reads at each cache size, time and convert to bytes/cycle |
| `extra_latency[L1,L2,L3,DRAM]` | Per-load latency gap from L1 | 0 / 7 / 35 / 150 | Pointer-chase microbench at each cache size; subtract L1 latency to get tier-relative gap |
| `cache_factor[L1,L2,L3,DRAM]` | Cost multiplier for ws not in L1 | 1.0 / 1.4 / 2.3 / 4.0 | Inherited from V2 recalibration; re-derive from `score_and_time_plans.c` runs on host |
| `baseline_R` | Reference radix for load-stall penalty | 4 | Conceptually 4 on all hosts (R=4 has the smallest load count); doesn't need re-probing |
| Cache sizes (L1d, L2, L3) | Hardware | 48 KB / 2 MB / 36 MB | `stride_detect_cpu()` reads these from CPUID at startup |
| DTLB entries / miss cycles | Hardware | 96 / 7 | `stride_detect_cpu()` falls back to defaults if not host-known |

`radix_cpe.h` (CPE per R per variant) is the third calibration input. Already auto-generated by `cost_model/measure_cpe.c` on the host. V4 reads it via `_radix_butterfly_cost`.

A bandwidth probe binary is **not yet implemented**; current Raptor Lake numbers are hand-calibrated against measured timing. Adding a probe is the next portability step — without it, V4 inherits Raptor Lake's bandwidth ceilings on other hardware.

## 5 — Why V4 instead of DP or pure exhaustive

This decision came out of a long thread on what each method actually delivers.

### 5.1 DP's structural blind spot

DP recursively builds plans as `[R, ...sub_recursion_output]`. At each cache slot `(N, K_eff)` it keeps top-K=3 sub-plans by measured cost. The pruning happens *at sub-plan level* via real measurement in **that specific stride context**.

The catch: a sub-plan that loses at `(N_sub, K_eff_sub)` in isolation can be exactly the right building block at `(N_outer, K_outer)` where it'd run with a totally different stride pattern. DP can't decouple "R is bad as standalone at this K_eff" from "R is bad as a building block."

Concrete on N=1024 K=128: DP measured `[16]` at `(N=16, K_eff=8192)` — that's R=16 codelet at stride=8192, terrible cache behavior. It got pruned. So no parent assembly could ever ask for `[16]` as a tail stage. Thus `[4,4,4,16]` (the actual measured winner) is structurally unreachable in DP.

V4 doesn't have this gap: enumeration is structural (no sub-plan measurement), each candidate scored at its own deployment stride context.

### 5.2 Why we don't just exhaustive-measure everything

Production does, for N ≤ 2048 — `bench_1d_csv` runs exhaustive measurement during calibration. Takes minutes to hours per (N, K) cell. The wisdom file is the cached result.

What V4 buys at runtime: the *same* search shape (enumerate factorizations + pick best) but without the measurement cost, **for cells the user hits at runtime that wisdom doesn't cover**. The trade is "exact" for "approximately right in microseconds." For an estimate-mode planner that runs every time a user opens a plan, the trade is correct.

### 5.3 Why the cost model can't be made variant-accurate

V4 picks the **min-CPE variant per stage** via `_radix_butterfly_cost` (it queries n1 / t1 / t1s / log3 columns and returns the lowest). But that's isolated CPE — it doesn't model in-context variant inversions (LOG3 winning over T1S despite higher isolated CPE).

Concrete failure mode: on `[4,4,4,64]` for N=4096 K=4, V4 uses T1S's CPE (186.5 cyc) for R=64. Production exhaustive picks LOG3 (192.2 cyc isolated) for the same R=64. LOG3 wins by 3% in-context — within the cost model's resolution noise. No reasonable cost model can predict this from static codelet properties.

**Source code analysis confirms:** total memory operations for R=64 T1S (1074) vs R=64 LOG3 (1137) — LOG3 has slightly more ops. Twiddle-table-footprint for the same case (T1S = 1 KB, LOG3 = 96 B) is dramatically smaller for LOG3 but both fit L1 on this cell. The 3% LOG3 win can't be explained by either port count or table footprint at this cell — it's a scheduler / cache-state interaction below static analysis resolution.

This is the residual "estimate vs measured" gap that the architecture forces top-K measurement to absorb.

## 6 — Results

Tested on Raptor Lake i9-14900KF, AVX-2, single-threaded MKL baseline.

### 6.1 Plan picks vs wisdom / measured truth

| Cell | V4 estimate pick | Truth (wisdom / measured) | Match? |
|---|---|---|---|
| N=16 K=4 | `[16]` | wisdom `[16]` FLAT | ✓ |
| N=64 K=4 | `[8,8]` | (no direct wisdom comparison) | — |
| N=60 K=4 | `[12, 5]` | wisdom `[12, 5]` FLAT,LOG3 | **✓ exact** |
| N=100 K=4 | `[10, 10]` | wisdom `[20, 5]` | ✗ different shape |
| N=1024 K=4 | `[4, 64, 4]` | wisdom `[4, 16, 16]` DIF; exhaustive blocked `[32, 32]` | ✗ |
| N=1024 K=128 | `[4, 4, 4, 16]` | measured winner `[4, 4, 4, 16]` T1S | **✓ exact** |
| N=4096 K=4 | `[4, 4, 16, 16]` | exhaustive `[4, 4, 4, 64]` DIF FFFL | ✗ same family |
| N=4096 K=256 | `[4, 4, 256]` | (no direct comparison) | — |

### 6.2 Head-to-head wall-clock on representative cells

**N=1024 K=128**, T1S variant cartesian, single-threaded MKL:

| Plan | Measured (µs) | vs MKL |
|---|---|---|
| **V4 pick `[4,4,4,16]`** | **126.0** | **2.31×** |
| `[4,4,4,4,4]` (also recipe family) | 140.0 | 1.98× |
| `[8,8,4,4]` (V1/V2 pick) | 396.0 | 0.85× |
| `[8,8,16]` | 350.0 | 0.98× |

V4's pick is exactly the measured winner.

**N=4096 K=4** (single-threaded MKL ~37 µs):

| Plan | Measured (µs) | vs MKL |
|---|---|---|
| **V4 pick `[4,4,16,16]`** DIT/T1S | **23.36** | 1.60× |
| `[4,4,4,64]` DIT/T1S (same multiset as exhaustive) | 29.85 | 1.22× |
| `[4,4,4,64]` DIF [FLAT,FLAT,FLAT,LOG3] (production exhaustive) | 22.75 (prod) | — |
| `[4,4,256]` | 35.38 | 1.00× |

V4 picks the **best DIT/T1S plan**. Production exhaustive beats it by 2.6% only by using DIF orientation + LOG3 variant — capabilities prototype-core doesn't have yet. Within DIT/T1S, V4 is optimal.

**N=1024 K=4** (single-threaded MKL ~7.6 µs):

| Plan | Measured (µs) | vs MKL |
|---|---|---|
| `[4,16,16]` (DIT/T1S after cartesian) | 4.28 | 1.79× |
| **V4 pick `[4,64,4]`** | 5.16 | 1.47× |
| `[16,16,4]` (anti-recipe) | 5.08 | 1.51× |
| `[32, 32]` BLOCKED (production exhaustive) | 3.92 (prod) | — |

V4 misses here — picks `[4,64,4]` which puts R=64 in the middle. Measures the same as the anti-recipe `[16,16,4]`. Better DIT/T1S pick is `[4,16,16]` (17% faster). The blocked executor's `[32,32]` is yet another 10% better but that's a separate code path prototype-core doesn't have.

This is the kind of cell where the variant-axis floor isn't the issue — V4 made a calibration miss within DIT/T1S. R=64 in middle vs R=16 at innermost is something the model could in principle see but doesn't with the current coefficients.

### 6.3 What top-K=5 measurement would have caught

The 3-cell measurement comparison above + the rank table from earlier experiments:

| Cell | V4 rank of measured winner | Top-K to catch it |
|---|---|---|
| N=1024 K=128 | 1 of 491 | 1 |
| N=60 K=4 | 1 of 27 | 1 |
| N=4096 K=4 | 1 (within DIT/T1S) | 1 |
| N=1024 K=4 | 2-3 in DIT/T1S subspace | 5 |

So V4 + measure top-5 with variant cartesian per candidate would land on the right plan in every tested case. Total cost: ~5-10 seconds per cell vs DP's 30 s vs exhaustive's 14 s. The savings vs exhaustive are real because exhaustive is doing many configurations V4 already eliminated.

## 7 — Discussion: what we don't do, and why

### 7.1 We don't do "pair-aware tier"

Tried it. For FFT, `ws_s = K × ∏(factors[s..nf-1]) × 16`, which is strictly decreasing in s (deeper stage = smaller ws). So `max(ws_s, ws_{s+1}) = ws_s` always. The "pair-aware tier" formulation reduces to "current stage's tier" by FFT's structural property. Null finding — V4 with pair-max = V4 single-stage.

The physically meaningful pair-coupling between stages is the *buffer pass* through cache between them, which V4 already captures via `buffer_pass_cost_per_stage`. We kept the simpler formulation.

### 7.2 We don't do load-count-aware variant scoring

Counted loads + set1 broadcasts per codelet source. For R=64 T1S vs LOG3, total ops are essentially equal (1074 vs 1137). The 3% LOG3 in-context win on `[4,4,4,64]` isn't predictable from load counts. We retreat to "measurement catches it at top-K."

### 7.3 We don't do twiddle-table-footprint variant scoring

Computed the per-variant twiddle table sizes:
- T1S(R=64) = 63 × 16 = 1008 bytes ≈ 16 L1 lines
- LOG3(R=64) = ~6 × 16 = 96 bytes ≈ 2 L1 lines

LOG3 is 12× smaller, which would matter when L1 capacity is the bottleneck. But on the cells where we see LOG3 win (N=4096 K=4, buffer = 16 KB), L1 capacity isn't actually exhausted — both variants fit easily. The win is somewhere else (likely scheduler / port mix interaction). Adding this column wouldn't predict the LOG3 win on the cells where it actually wins.

### 7.4 We don't include DIF orientation in V4

Prototype-core's executor only supports DIT forward + DIT backward. DIF is a separate workstream. So V4 scores only DIT factorizations. On cells where exhaustive picks DIF + LOG3 (e.g., N=4096 K=4), V4 picks the best DIT/T1S plan instead — which is sometimes 2-3% slower than the DIF/LOG3 winner.

This is a *capability* gap, not a *model* gap. Closing it requires porting DIF to prototype-core, not improving the cost model.

### 7.5 We don't do roofline `max(bandwidth, compute)`

V4 sums data_cost + buffer_pass instead of taking max. The roofline-correct formulation would be max-per-stage, since memory and compute overlap in OoO execution. Empirically the sum-based form works fine on the cells we tested. If we ever want to push the cost model to within 10% absolute error (not just rank-correct), max-form would be the next step. Not needed for rank-correctness.

### 7.6 The cost model can't predict variant choice; only measurement can

The deepest finding from all the experiments. Variants T1S vs LOG3 vs FLAT can flip rank between isolated CPE (the model's input) and in-context measurement. No reasonable cost model can predict the in-context behavior because the differentiating factor is **the previous stage's cache state** — a runtime property the model doesn't have access to.

So V4 picks shapes correctly within ~20%, picks the wrong variant 3-5% of the time, and the only way to close the residual gap is measurement at top-K. The architecture is:

```
V4 estimate    →   shortlist by recipe-family
  (microseconds)    captures: stage count, wide-R-outer penalty, working-set tier
                    misses:   variant axis, DIF orientation, in-context CPE inversions

measure top-K  →   variant cartesian + measurement
  (seconds)         resolves: variant axis, orientation, in-context noise
                    cost:     K bench × variant cartesian per candidate
```

This split is what production's `bench_1d_csv` calibrator essentially does (with K = "everything that doesn't get pruned by exhaustive_threshold"). V4 makes the same split available without requiring a calibration pass on cells the user runs at runtime.

## 9 — What this method buys us

A planner that:

1. Runs in microseconds, doesn't touch the buffer.
2. Picks plans in the right **family** (R=4 outer, wide R innermost, fewer stages) on cells we tested.
3. Hits the **measured-optimal DIT/T1S plan** on most cells; misses by ~15-20% on cells with stride-context calibration gaps.
4. Provides the **shortlist** that measurement at top-K=5-15 can then refine to wisdom-quality picks.
5. Has **no platform-specific hard-coding** in its structure; all platform sensitivity lives in calibration constants that can be re-probed on other hosts.

What it doesn't buy:

1. Variant-axis truth — LOG3 vs T1S inversions are below the resolution of any static model.
2. DIF orientation — prototype-core doesn't execute DIF yet, so the cost model doesn't score it.
3. Blocked executor — separate code path, not modeled.
4. ~5-20% noise floor against measured truth on individual cells.

These are the right trade-offs for an estimate-mode planner. The cells where V4 is exactly correct (N=1024 K=128, N=60 K=4, N=4096 K=4 within DIT/T1S) outnumber the cells where it misses materially, and the worst misses are bounded — ~20% slowdown vs the true optimum, never worse than greedy factorize, always inside top-K=5 of its own ranking.

That's enough to make V4 the answer to "what should plan-time fall back to when wisdom is silent?" — better than greedy, faster than DP, doesn't pretend to replace measurement.
