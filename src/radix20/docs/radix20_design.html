01 — Decomposition

4×5 Cooley-Tukey

DFT-20 = 5 × DFT-4 → internal W₂₀ twiddles → 4 × DFT-5. The 4×5 factoring (not 5×4) is chosen because DFT-4 needs only 8 registers (4 complex) while DFT-5 needs 12 (5 complex + temps). Putting the smaller butterfly first keeps peak register pressure low throughout.

Phase 1
5× DFT-4
Radix-4 butterfly. Peak 8 regs per sub-FFT. Results → L1 spill buffer.
Twiddle
W₂₀ Internal
12 non-trivial entries. XOR negation for W⁵(×−j), W¹⁰(×−1), W¹⁵(×j). 6 regs peak.
Phase 2
4× DFT-5
Rader butterfly. Constants from memory (not regs). Peak 12 regs per sub-FFT.

Why Constants from Memory?

The DFT-5 Rader butterfly uses 4 constants (cA=0.559, cB=0.951, cC=0.588, cD=0.25). Hoisting them into YMM registers would consume 4 of the 16 available, leaving only 12 for data — not enough. Instead we emit _mm256_set1_pd(literal) which compiles to vbroadcastsd from .rodata. Cost: ~4 cycle L1 hit per constant access. Savings: 4 free YMM registers, which means zero compiler spills.

02 — Register Budgeting

Zero Spills, by Design

The central insight: FFT codelets cannot afford compiler register spills. A spill costs 32 bytes to L1 (store) + 32 bytes back (reload) at ~4 cycles each. With 170+ spills (as measured in the DAG codelet), that's 1300+ wasted cycles per k-iteration — more than the butterfly computation itself.

03 — Twiddle Strategies

Multi-Tier Twiddle Architecture

Three twiddle strategies, each optimal at a different K range. The planner selects the winner per-K at plan time.

Contiguous Flat

19 entries packed per k-group of VL. Sequential loads, perfect prefetch. Best at K ≤ 32 where the 19×VL block fits L1.

Binary Ladder

5 base loads (W¹, W², W⁴, W⁸, W¹⁶) + W³ precompute + 14 cmul derivations. Saves 14 strided loads at the cost of 14 FMAs. Wins at K ≥ 64.

Buffered Output

DAG writes to 1.3 KB stack buffer, then one scatter pass. Collapses 40 KB write working set at K=256 to L1. Sweet spot: exactly K=256.

Internal W₂₀ Twiddles

8 unique non-trivial exponents from the set {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 12}. Four special cases get fast paths:

04 — DAG vs CT Crossover

Two Butterflies, One Dispatch

The genfft DAG (208 ops, Frigo & Johnson) has fewer arithmetic operations than the CT (272 ops). But the DAG's 81 simultaneous live variables cause 128+ compiler spills on AVX2. At low K the spills hide in L1 latency; at high K they compete with data loads for L1 bandwidth. The dispatch threshold is calibrated per-hardware.

05 — AVX-512 ILP Pairing

Two FMA Ports, Always Fed

A single DFT-5 stream can't saturate both FMA ports (p0/p1 on Intel) because of data dependencies — each FMA depends on the previous add/sub. Two interleaved streams keep both ports fed. Measured 1.25–2.03× over our own AVX2 CT.

06 — Split vs Native IL

Two Formats, Pipeline-Aware

Split format stores real and imaginary parts in separate arrays. Native interleaved (IL) stores them alternating: [re₀, im₀, re₁, im₁, …]. IL gets free ×j rotations via addsub/fmaddsub but halves the k-step (2 DFTs per YMM instead of 4).

08 — Fair Same-ISA Benchmarks

Honest Numbers, No ISA Mismatch

Two separate builds — one compiled with AVX2-only (no AVX-512 available), one with AVX-512 — so FFTW's planner is forced to pick from the same ISA tier we use.

Key Finding: FFTW Never Uses AVX-512 for R=20

Even with AVX-512 available, FFTW's planner picks n1fv_20_avx (128-bit!) for almost every K. Their genfft DAG has 53 interleaved variables — too many for 32 ZMM when combined with shuffle/permute temporaries. The planner measures all ISA variants and picks 128-bit as fastest. This means our ILP-paired AVX-512 CT is a genuine architectural advantage over FFTW — not an ISA mismatch.

Analysis

K ≤ 256: We Win (both ISAs)

Compute-bound. Our zero-spill CT + genfft DAG (208 ops) beat FFTW's sequential DAG (53 IL vars → spills on all ISA widths). AVX2: 1.07–1.51×. AVX-512: 1.15–1.31×.

K = 256–2048: AVX-512 Extends the Win

L2-bound. AVX2 loses at K=512 (0.93×), but AVX-512 ILP pairing still wins at K=1024 (1.05×). The extra throughput from dual FMA streams buys ~4× more headroom before bandwidth bottleneck.

K ≥ 4096: FFTW Wins (bandwidth)

L3/DRAM-bound. FFTW's native interleaved format: 20 complex loads + 20 stores = 40 streams. Our split format: 20 re loads + 20 im loads + 20 re stores + 20 im stores = 80 streams. 2× memory traffic at the bandwidth wall. Fix: use our IL codelet at high K.

FFTW Planner Behavior

BuildFFTW PicksWhy
AVX2 buildn1fv_20_avx (128-bit)53 IL vars → 128-bit has fewest spills
AVX-512 buildn1fv_20_avx (128-bit)Same — planner rejects avx512 codelet

FFTW's genfft DAG for R=20 has 53 interleaved variables. At 256-bit (AVX2), that's 53 YMM → 37 spills minimum. At 512-bit (AVX-512), it's 53 ZMM → 21 spills, but each spill is 64 bytes instead of 32. The planner's measurement says 128-bit (where each spill is only 16 bytes) wins. Our CT architecture avoids this entirely — peak 12 regs per sub-FFT, zero compiler spills at any ISA width.

Planner Strategy Map

The simulated planner selects 4 different strategies across K, each optimal in its regime:

K = 4–32: Split DAG

Compute-bound, L1-resident. The genfft DAG's 208 ops beats CT's 272. Spills hidden by L1 latency. 1.24–1.45× vs FFTW.

K = 64: IL NOTW

IL format's ×j via addsub (free) and half the load streams starts to win. 1.40× vs FFTW.

K = 128–256: Split CT / DAG

L2 transition. AVX-512 ILP-paired CT wins at K=256 (1.29×), DAG still wins at K=128 (1.80×).

K ≥ 512: IL NOTW

Bandwidth-bound. IL format halves memory streams (20 vs 40). ILP-paired butterfly still fills both FMA ports. 1.21–1.47× vs FFTW through to DRAM.
